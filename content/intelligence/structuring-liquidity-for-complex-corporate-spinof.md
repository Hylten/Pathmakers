# Structuring Liquidity for Complex Corporate Spinoffs

Corporate spinoffs are rarely graceful events.  

They break systems.  

They expose balance sheets that were carried for too long.  

They force operators and investors to confront the structural realities of assets that can no longer hide behind consolidated reporting.  



In the mid‑market, especially in healthcare where carve‑outs often contain legacy reimbursements, aging AR, and operational drag, liquidity is the oxygen that determines whether a spinoff stabilizes or collapses.  

This is where Pathmaker operates.  

Not as a turnaround shop.  

Not as an advisor searching for narrative redemption.  

But as a silent authority that structures liquidity with precision for those who already understand what is at stake.  



## The Hard Truth About Mid‑Market Spinoffs

Most corporate spinoffs are not strategic masterpieces.  

They are cleanup events.  

Mid‑market private equity firms face reimbursement cliffs, outdated EHR stacks, or service line portfolios that ballooned without discipline.  

Healthcare decision cycles are slow.  

Working capital is inconsistent.  

Lenders dislike ambiguity, and ambiguity is exactly what a carve‑out produces.  



Traditional credit sources typically retreat.  

They want audited financials that do not exist yet.  

They want predictable EBITDA that the spinoff has not stabilized.  

They want entity‑level collateral that is still in transition.  



In that void, liquidity must come from someone who can underwrite the assets as they are, not as the projections claim they will be.  



## Why Liquidity Structuring Matters More Than Ever

Spinoffs generate temporal dislocation.  

Receivables shift.  

Supply chain terms change.  

Vendor contracts need novation.  

Payroll and insurance move to new entities.  



Even sophisticated HNWI and UHNW operators underestimate how quickly a spinoff can become illiquid simply because timing mismatches compound.  

Two weeks of lag in reimbursement.

Four weeks of aging AR review.

Sixty days of legal transition before assets can be perfected.  



This lag is fatal if liquidity is not staged correctly.  



Pathmaker structures capital so the spinoff remains solvent through every transition phase.  

Not by promising miracles.  

But by building mechanical certainty into an inherently uncertain environment.

## The Liquidity Stack for Complex Carve‑Outs

Liquidity in a spinoff should never be a single source.  

It is a stack.  

A staged architecture that flexes as assets transition, operational clarity increases, and risk decays.  



A typical Pathmaker liquidity stack includes:  

• ABL against transitional or unseasoned receivables  

• Private credit secured by defined cash flows or asset pools  

• Supplemental liquidity via crypto lending when digital assets are involved, minimum threshold $2,000,000  

• Public share lending for investors with concentrated equity positions, minimum threshold $5,000,000  



Each layer provides a different form of control, velocity, and insulation.  

Together, they create a buffer that absorbs the turbulence inherent in a spinoff.

## The Pressure Points Unique to Healthcare Carve‑Outs

Healthcare spinoffs are structurally more complicated than those in other sectors.  

Revenue cycles are regulated.  

Reimbursements are contested.  

AR quality decays faster than anyone admits.  

Compliance adds friction to every transaction.  



During a carve‑out, typical pressure points include:  

• Reprocessing claims under new entity IDs  
• Payer contracts requiring renegotiation  
• Coding backlogs created by staffing gaps  
• Cash flow troughs during billing transitions  
• Legacy liabilities that courts or counterparties attempt to reassign  



Many operators believe they can manage these internally.  

Some can.  

Most cannot without external liquidity that can pivot fast and without constraint.  



## Timing and Sequencing: The Hidden Drivers of Spinoff Success

In every carve‑out we have supported, the determining factor was not strategy or management talent.  

It was timing and sequencing.  



If liquidity arrives before operational disruption, the spinoff stabilizes.  

If it arrives after, the operator spends the next twelve months in defensive mode.  



Proper sequencing requires liquidity that is:  

• Available before asset schedules fully finalize  
• Deployable even when financials are partially reconciled  
• Structured around transitional volatility rather than penalizing it  



This is the advantage of private credit combined with ABL.  

We can structure against imperfect information as long as the asset base and risk profile are fundamentally real.

## Avoiding the Three Classic Spinoff Failures

After years of handling mid‑market carve‑outs, three failures appear consistently.  

They are predictable.  

They are preventable.  



• **Underestimating cash flow disruption**  
Executives model accounts receivable as if payer behavior remains unchanged. It never does.  

• **Delaying liquidity until after the carve‑out**  
By the time the new entity begins billing, the liquidity window has already tightened.  

• **Using capital structures that cannot flex**  
A single lender with rigid requirements will stall the spinoff the moment variables shift.  



Pathmaker structures liquidity intentionally to avoid all three.
