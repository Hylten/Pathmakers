# The Role of ABL in Private Equity Carve-Outs

Private equity carve-outs are not elegant deals. They are operational divorces carried out under time pressure, incomplete information, and conflicting incentives.

They rarely resemble strategic transformations. They function more like emergency engineering projects. The parent company wants the assets gone. The buyer wants the assets isolated. The clock is always ticking.

In this environment, capital structure determines survivability.

Asset-Based Lending, when used correctly, becomes the stabilizing spine of the transaction. It provides liquidity that is measurable, collateralized, and friction resistant. It also creates tactical freedom for the sponsor who needs to execute an accelerated operational stand up.

This is where Pathmaker operates. Silent authority. We provide the structural mechanics for institutional capital.



## Why ABL Fits the Realities of Mid-Market Carve-Outs

Most mid-market deals sit in an uncomfortable zone. The assets are meaningful enough to attract private equity attention, but not extraordinary enough for lenders to assume low risk.

Traditional cash flow financing often fails here. Not because the target is dysfunctional, but because carve-outs violate underwriting comfort.

There are no clean historicals.

There are no clean allocations of overhead.

There is no standalone EBITDA that behaves like a predictable metric.

ABL avoids this financial debris field because it is collateral focused. Instead of relying on pro forma illusions, it looks directly at working assets.

Inventory.

Receivables.

Equipment.

In carve-outs, these are the only numbers that matter because they are the only numbers that can be proven.



## ABL as a Liquidity Engine During Transitional Chaos

A carve-out is a liquidity stress test. It is not unusual for a newly separated entity to suffer:

Operational downtime.

Vendor friction.

Unexpected working capital spikes.

IT cutovers that break revenue cycles.

Management teams that inherit responsibility without authority.

ABL delivers a liquidity buffer that absorbs this chaos. It gives the sponsor time. It gives the operators room to execute. It prevents value erosion that happens when a standalone business struggles with cash timing.

ABL is not a luxury. It is a hydraulic system that keeps the business upright while the new foundation is built.



## ABL and the Separation Timeline

The timeline of a carve-out is never clean. There are usually three distinct phases.

Separation.

Stand up.

Stabilization.

ABL liquidity behaves differently across each phase, and understanding this is the difference between a stable transaction and a fragile one.



### Separation Phase

This phase is always disorderly.

The target is leaving the parent ecosystem.

SLAs are negotiated under pressure.

Employees are repositioned.

IT systems are partitioned.

During this period, ABL functions as an immediate liquidity injection. It plugs the holes left behind by the parent’s treasury function. It restores working capital continuity.

Without this buffer, even healthy carve-outs experience short term distress.



### Stand Up Phase

This is the most capital inefficient period of the lifecycle.

A standalone business must build infrastructure that is not yet revenue producing:

HR  
Finance  
ERP  
Supply chain  
Compliance  

The sponsor needs liquidity here that can be deployed flexibly. Not delayed. Not conditional on performance milestones. ABL delivers this because it is collateral driven and not projection driven.

This phase is where private credit lenders often tighten. ABL becomes the reliable counterweight.



### Stabilization Phase

By now, the business operates independently.

Revenue cycles normalize.

Gross margins become observable.

Management becomes accountable to real metrics.

At this point, the ABL line becomes a predictable working capital utility. It remains in place not as an emergency tool, but as a structural cost of doing business.

Private credit can layer on top of this. In fact, this is where Pathmaker’s dual competency matters. We lend against private credit and asset-based structures. The integration creates a capital stack that can handle volatility without sacrificing optionality.



## Why Healthcare Carve-Outs Need ABL Even More

Healthcare systems and related providers represent some of the most complex carve-outs in the mid-market.

The assets are operationally integrated but legally fragmented.

Billing cycles are slow.

Reimbursement models are inconsistent.

Working capital swings are violent and non-negotiable.

Liquefying these assets under private credit alone is difficult. The underwriting timeline is long. The documentation requirements are heavy. The performance risk is elevated.

ABL fits the sector because it ignores the narrative. It focuses on the collateral. Healthcare receivables remain predictable enough for an experienced lender to create a stable borrowing base.

For sponsors executing provider group rollups or divestitures from hospital systems, this precision matters. ABL becomes the only facility that can be deployed at the pace required for these transactions.



## Carve-Out Challenges That ABL Neutralizes

ABL does not solve every issue, but it neutralizes several of the most destructive ones.



### 1. The Absence of Standalone EBITDA

An ABL lender does not need a full income statement reconstruction. The lender needs access to the collateral. This is the reason carve-outs often qualify for ABL even when they are not eligible for cash flow solutions.



### 2. Vendor Skepticism After the Separation

Vendors often panic when a business leaves its parent.

ABL lines provide the proof of liquidity that stabilizes vendor terms. It signals that the new business is financed and credible.



### 3. Cash Conversion Uncertainty

During the first 180 days, cash conversion cycles behave unpredictably.

Receivables slow.

Inventory moves erratically.

Payable cycles shorten.

ABL gives sponsors a buffer that prevents operational disruptions during this volatility.



### 4. Parent Company Transitional Services Agreement Gaps

TSAs almost always fail in one or more areas.

If the carve-out misses a billing cycle or loses access to the parent’s cash management system, ABL bridges the gap with actionable liquidity.
